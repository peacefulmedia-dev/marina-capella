Homepage — the thinking behind it

A walk through the strategy, craft, and accessibility decisions behind the homepage concept — so the team can review the staging site knowing what each choice is trying to do, and why.

Staging: mc.peacefuldesign.com Concept: Homepage 1 · v4.3 Status: Review — interim photography

Marina isn't one business — she's a physician, a mentor, a nonprofit founder, an author, and a clinic owner. The hardest design problem here wasn't making it pretty; it was making one page hold all of that without feeling scattered. Everything below serves a single organizing idea: “healing the healer” — a calm, human, unmistakably-not-corporate presence that gives every kind of visitor a clear path in.

01

Strategy first — one purpose, many avenues

Marina serves four distinct audiences who'd normally need four different sites: physicians facing burnout, future doctors from underrepresented backgrounds, families seeking whole-person care, and event organizers. Rather than flatten those into a generic “services” list, the page leads with her story and then fans out into clearly-labeled avenues.

  • The hero makes a promise, not a pitch. “Healing the healer. Reimagining medicine.” states the worldview before any ask — it earns trust before it earns a click.
  • The “avenues” section sorts visitors by who they are — For physicians / For future doctors / For families — so each person self-selects in seconds and routes out to the right property (DPC Pediatrician, Future Minority Doctor, Healing Arts Pediatrics).
  • Conversion is sequenced, not stacked. Soft entry (book mailing list) up top, deeper commitments (coaching, speaking, becoming a patient) further down, once she's earned the scroll.
02

The palette — warmth with a clinical backbone

A disciplined five-color system, built from the client-approved swatch. The governing rule: cream everywhere, never stark white — so the page feels like paper and skin rather than a clinic waiting room. Deep forest grounds it; terracotta is reserved exclusively for things you can click.

Why two steps of some colors? The approved sage and terracotta are gorgeous at display size but too light for small text on cream. So each has a deepened step used only where real text sits on it — same hue, accessible contrast. Nothing was invented; the brand palette was tuned, not replaced.
03

Typography — the science and the soul

Two families, a deliberate duet. Fraunces — a warm, literary serif with true italics — carries every headline and pull-quote; it does the emotional, human work. Figtree — a clean, friendly grotesque — handles body copy and UI; it does the clear, trustworthy work. That pairing is Marina's thesis (integrate left brain and right) expressed in type.

04

Layout & rhythm — room to breathe

The page alternates between airy cream sections and three deep-sage panels (video, speaking, contact). That cadence does two jobs: it paces a long scroll so it never feels like a wall of text, and it gives the most emotional moments — her voice, her stage, the invitation to connect — a darker, quieter room to land in.

  • A soft top-light wash warms the top of each cream section and settles back to base — a subtle breath of light that only ever goes lighter, so it never harms legibility.
  • The full-bleed hero places Marina to the right, facing into the headline. The photo is intentionally mirrored and a precision-tuned scrim keeps the text column dark enough to read over any part of the image — desktop and mobile use different scrim geometry so contrast holds at every width.
  • Consistent ornament: a small ochre diamond divider and 7px sage marks recur throughout, stitching the sections into one identity.
05

Motion — calm by default

The interaction layer is deliberately restrained — this is a healer's site, not a startup landing page. Movement is gentle, purposeful, and always optional. Every effect is gated behind both the OS “reduce motion” setting and an internal calm-mode switch.

Staggered reveals

Content fades up gently as it enters view, with cards cascading a beat apart — guiding the eye down the page.

Hero parallax

The hero image and text drift at slightly different speeds on scroll — depth without distraction.

Count-up stats & magnetic buttons

Numbers tick up when seen; primary CTAs lean subtly toward the cursor — small moments of life.

Card tilt & divider draw-in

Avenue cards respond to the pointer with a soft 3-D tilt; dividers draw themselves in.

Accessibility is built into the motion, not bolted on. Touch devices skip the pointer effects entirely, and anyone with “reduce motion” enabled gets a calm, static page with full content — never a blank screen waiting on an animation.
06

Conversion craft — the contact gatekeeper

Because Marina wears so many hats, an open “contact us” box would be chaos. Instead the form opens with one question — “I'm reaching out about…” — and adapts. Choose “becoming a patient” or “DPC consulting” and a gentle note points you straight to the right property before you even send a message. Her team gets pre-sorted inquiries; visitors get an instant answer.

  • Self-routing intent deflects the inquiries that have a better home elsewhere, and cleanly tags the ones that should reach her team.
  • The “Inquire about speaking” button pre-fills that intent — the page remembers context as you move through it.
07

Accessibility — a non-negotiable, audited

For a physician's brand, accessibility isn't a checkbox — it's a values statement. The whole palette was tuned to WCAG 2.1 AA, with every text-on-color pairing measured rather than eyeballed.

  • Measured contrast everywhere — the deepened color steps exist specifically so small text always clears AA, even over the hero photo's brightest regions.
  • Full keyboard support — a skip link, visible focus rings tuned per background (dark rings on cream, light rings on the sage/forest panels), and proper labels on every field.
  • Semantic, screen-reader-friendly HTML — real landmarks, descriptive alt text, and live-region hints on the contact form's routing notes.
  • Respects the user's system — reduced-motion users get a complete, calm experience with zero functionality lost.
08

Build & performance — fast, simple, durable

The site is plain HTML, CSS, and a little vanilla JavaScript — no framework, no build step, nothing to break in two years. It deploys as static files (currently on Vercel) and is cheap to host and trivial to hand off.

  • Click-to-play video facade — the page shows a lightweight thumbnail and only loads the YouTube player on click, so nothing heavy loads up front (and it uses the privacy-friendly no-cookie embed).
  • Images are modern WebP and lazy-loaded below the fold; the hero has a forest-colored fallback so there's never a flash of empty space.
  • Progressive enhancement — the content and links all work even if JavaScript never runs.
09

What's interim — and what's next

So the team reviews with the right lens, here's what is deliberately placeholder on the staging site:

  • Photography — the hero, headshot, and portrait are interim images sourced from existing sites. They're standing in for the final brand shoot, and are marked as such on the page.
  • The video — currently an existing “Burnout to Breakthrough” interview; to be replaced by the Peaceful Media video team's brand piece.
  • Testimonials — patient quotes are real (from Healing Arts Pediatrics); physician-coaching and mentee testimonials still to be gathered.
  • The book cover — a typographic concept, not final cover art.
Everything structural — layout, palette, type, interaction, accessibility — is review-ready. The open items are content (photos, video, quotes) that drop into finished slots. Review the system; the assets will follow.
